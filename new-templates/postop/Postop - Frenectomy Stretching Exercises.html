Frenectomy Stretching Exercises
Post-Operative Instructions

Why This Matters

Your body's natural healing response is to close the wound by pulling its edges back together. Without anything pulling it the other way, the frenum will grow back to its original position over the first few weeks of healing. Stretching keeps the wound edges separated during healing so the new tissue forms in a position that releases the frenum permanently.

The exercises matter more than anything else about your recovery. If you only remember one thing from your post-op instructions, remember this.

When to Start, When to Stop

  • Start: the day after surgery, at the next meal or bedtime that falls 24 hours after your appointment.
  • Continue: for 3 to 4 weeks, or until Dr. Whatcott clears you at your follow-up appointment.
  • Do not stop early just because the area looks healed. The surface heals before the underlying tissue stops trying to reattach.

How Often

Perform the exercise 4 times each day: with each meal, plus before bed.

The Exercise

  1. Wash your hands.
  2. Stand in front of a mirror with good light.
  3. Use your thumb and index finger (or both thumbs) to pull your lower lip down and out, exposing the surgical site fully. The diamond-shaped wound should open up visibly.
  4. Hold the lip in this stretched position for 3 to 5 seconds, then release.
  5. Repeat 5 times per session.
  6. On the last repetition, keep the lip stretched and use a clean fingertip to gently sweep across the wound side-to-side once. This keeps the wound edges from sticking back together. The motion is across, not into the wound.
Total time per session: about 60 seconds. 4 sessions per day for 3 to 4 weeks. The whole protocol adds up to about 4 minutes of your day.

Comfort Tips

  • First 2 to 3 days: Time your exercises to land 30 to 60 minutes after a dose of ibuprofen and acetaminophen. The exercises will feel sore but should not be sharp.
  • A few drops of pinpoint bleeding during a stretch is normal and expected. It does not mean you are causing damage.
  • Rinse with warm salt water after each session.
  • Tenderness usually improves noticeably each day after day 3.

What You Should Not Do

  • Do not push your finger into the wound. The motion is across the surface, not down into it.
  • Do not skip days during the first week. The first 7 to 10 days are when reattachment is most likely.
  • Do not stop early. Stopping early is the most common reason frenectomies fail. Even if the area looks fully healed at week 2, the underlying tissue is still actively trying to reattach for the full 3 to 4 weeks.
  • Do not pick at the white or yellow film covering the wound. It protects the healing tissue and will fall away on its own.

Call Us If

  • Sharp pain during stretching does not improve after the first 3 to 4 days.
  • Bleeding during a stretch is more than a few drops or does not stop within a minute.
  • You see signs of infection: pus, foul taste, swelling that worsens after day 3, or fever above 101°F.
  • You are unsure whether what you are seeing is normal. We would rather check than have you guess.
