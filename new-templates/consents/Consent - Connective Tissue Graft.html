Diagnosis

In health, teeth are surrounded by two types of tissue: gingiva and mucosa. Gingiva is a firm, fibrous tissue that adheres well to the roots of the teeth and the underlying jawbone. Mucosa is softer, like cheek tissue, and does not hold as well. When mucosa is the primary tissue at the gumline, it is much more likely to recede, exposing more of the root and leading to jawbone loss around the tooth. A good amount of jawbone is essential, as it is the bone that holds the teeth in place.

After an examination and study of my dental condition, Dr. Whatcott has advised me that I have a defect of attached gingiva (firm gum tissue) around some teeth. With this condition, further recession may occur. Additionally, for fillings or crowns with edges under the gumline, it is important to have sufficient width of firm gingiva to withstand the irritation they may cause. Gingiva also improves appearance and protects the roots of the teeth.

Recommended Treatment

Dr. Whatcott has recommended that gingival grafting (gum grafting) be performed in some areas of my mouth. Local anesthetic will be administered as part of the surgery. Gum grafting involves transplanting a thin strip of gingiva from either the palate (roof of the mouth, where sutures will be placed) or human allograft donor tissue processed from a US-accredited tissue bank. The existing gum tissue will be prepared, and the transplanted graft will be sutured in place. Non-dissolvable sutures will need to be removed in about one week. Dissolvable sutures around the area will fall off in about three to four weeks.

In some cases, about three to four months after the graft is done, it is necessary to refine the grafted area, similar to a scar revision, usually using a laser. There is no extra fee for this.

Expected Benefits

The purpose of gingival grafting is to create an amount of attached gum tissue adequate to reduce the gum defect.

Important: Do not expect all of the exposed tooth root caused by existing gum recession to be totally recovered with this gum graft surgery. That may not happen.

Principal Risks and Complications

A small number of patients (usually around 5%) do not have the graft "take." The usual causes are excessive shrinking of the graft tissue during healing, smoking, or the patient knocking the graft loose during the first week. It may be necessary to repeat the graft after about three months to allow the surgical sites to heal first. Usually, but not always, the second graft will take.

Complications that may result from the surgery, regenerative materials, drugs, or anesthetics include, but are not limited to: post-surgical infection, bleeding, swelling, pain, facial bruising, jaw joint pain or muscle spasm, cracking or bruising of the corners of the mouth, restricted ability to open the mouth for several days or weeks, impact on speech, allergic reactions, accidental swallowing of foreign matter, and transient or permanent increase in tooth looseness, tooth sensitivity to hot, cold, sweet, or acidic foods, and transient (on rare occasions permanent) numbness of the jaw, lip, tongue, chin, or gums. The exact duration of any complication cannot be determined, and they may be irreversible.

Alternatives to Suggested Treatment

No treatment. The likelihood of gum recession and subsequent jawbone loss around the affected teeth is higher without a gum graft. Studies show the incidence of tooth loss is six times greater in individuals with untreated gum problems compared to early treatment.

It is important for me to continue to see my regular dentist for routine care. Smoking may adversely affect gum healing and limit the successful outcome of surgery. Studies show smokers have more grafts that fail to take than non-smokers. I have told Dr. Whatcott about any pertinent medical conditions, allergies, or medications I am taking.

I agree to:
