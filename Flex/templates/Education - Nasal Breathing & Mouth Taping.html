Nasal Breathing & Mouth Taping
Patient Education

At our office, we look at the "Big Picture" of your health. We've found that the first signs of how you breathe and sleep show up right here in your mouth. By identifying these risks early, we can help you breathe, sleep, and live better.

It's Not Just a "Sleep" Problem

Many people believe airway issues only happen at night. In reality, most patients have a 24-hour Airway Disorder that simply becomes more dangerous at night. The root cause is often "over-breathing" through the mouth, which acts like a slow form of hyperventilation.

Mouth Breathing vs. Nasal Breathing

  • Mouth Breathing: Moves a lot of air but is highly inefficient. It "blows off" too much CO₂ (carbon dioxide), which prevents your red blood cells from releasing oxygen to your brain and tissues. It keeps your body in a "stressed" (sympathetic) state.
  • Nasal Breathing: This is your body's natural filtration and delivery system. It ensures the right balance of CO₂ so that oxygen is delivered where it's needed (this is known as the Bohr Effect).

Your Nose: The Ultimate Filter

Nasal breathing does more than just move air. It filters pollutants, humidifies air to protect your lungs, and delivers oxygen efficiently. Most importantly, it produces Nitric Oxide (NO)—a "miracle molecule" that opens blood vessels and helps your immune system fight off viruses and bacteria.

"I can't breathe through my nose!"
We often can't breathe through our noses because we don't use them. Mouth breathing causes nasal passages to become inflamed. Once you commit to nasal breathing, the Nitric Oxide your body produces acts as a natural decongestant, "popping" the airway open and shrinking inflamed tissue over time.

What Does "Healthy Breathing" Look Like?

At rest, your breathing should be:

  • Nasal Only: Your mouth should be closed.
  • Silent: No audible gasping or heavy sighs.
  • Steady: Roughly 8–10 breaths per minute.
  • Diaphragmatic: Driven by your belly, not your chest or shoulders.

The Next Step: Nighttime Success

Since you cannot consciously control your breathing while asleep, we use specific tools to ensure your nose stays on the job all night long. We recommend a gentle, hypoallergenic surgical tape such as 3M Nexcare Micropore—a small vertical strip placed in the center of the lips is all you need. Some patients find it more comfortable to place the strip horizontally to cover the lips completely—try both and see what feels best for you.

Frequently Asked Questions About Mouth Taping

How do I get started?

Start by practicing during the day. Wear the tape for short periods while reading or watching TV so you can get comfortable breathing through your nose. Once that feels natural, try it for one night. Build up gradually from there—there is no rush.

Is it safe? Will I be able to breathe?

Yes. The goal is to encourage your body to use its natural filtration system—your nose. If your nose becomes blocked during the night, your body's natural "arousal response" will wake you up, or you will instinctively remove the tape or open your mouth. This is why we insist you practice while awake first to ensure your nasal passages are clear.

What if I have a cold or my allergies are acting up?

Do not use mouth tape if you are congested. If you cannot breathe comfortably through your nose while awake, skip the therapy until your nasal passages are clear. Nasal saline sprays or rinses can be helpful to use before bed to ensure a clear airway.

Will it hurt to take the tape off in the morning?

It shouldn't. If you find the tape too sticky, applying a small amount of lip balm before applying the tape will prevent it from sticking too firmly. You can also "tame" the adhesive by sticking the tape to the back of your hand once or twice before applying it. If needed, a little warm water in the morning helps loosen it, but this is rarely necessary.

How long does it take to notice a difference?

Many patients report feeling more refreshed and having less "morning brain fog" within the first 3 to 7 nights. Long-term benefits—such as reduced snoring and improved dental health (less dry mouth)—usually become more apparent after 2–3 weeks of consistent use.

What if I rip it off in my sleep?

This is very common during the first week! Don't be discouraged. It simply means your brain is still adjusting to the new breathing pattern. Just try again the next night.
